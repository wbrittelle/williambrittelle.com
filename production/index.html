PRODUCTION APPROACH + PROCESS

Although computers are useful to sketch ideas and edit material, my production approach is rooted in the physical world. I believe that getting the right people in the right room with the right equipment equals the best vibe, the most compelling performances, and generates the most exciting ideas. I primarily work out of figureight recordings, a two-floor bespoke studio in Prospect Heights, Brooklyn owned by Shahzad Ismaily. Over the last few decades, Shahzad has curated an incredible array of vintage equipment, including two wonderful boards, an amazing selection of guitars and synths, and loads of priceless, hand-selected outboard gear. My work at figureight and with New Amsterdam Records has helped me develop an extensive rolodex of wonderful musicians that can swing by for a couple hours (or days) to help fill out recording projects.

Making records isn't cheap, but I think one cost that's often overlooked is the time of the lead artist. I've found that often 1-2 days in the studio yields better results than weeks of hopelessly futzing around with plug-ins in Ableton. I regularly work with the figueright house engineer Michael Hammond, a gentle giant with phenomenal mixing skills. Often we'll track and pre-mix in the studio, print all the outboard gear, and then he'll continue tweaking the "long mix" from his home studio which helps reduce cost and maximize our studio time. We've also done work studio mixing projects that were largely recorded off site or at home. For artists who prefer more of a "retreat" format, I've worked often with the Grammy-winning magician Zach Hanson (Bon Iver, yMusic, the Staves, S. Carey) at the HIVE, a beautiful and extremely quaint studio in a field in Eau Claire, Wisconsin filled with fossils, old books, great synths, hand-picked snacks and amazing vibes.

Should it prove useful, I also have extensive experience with arranging (more info on that here) and am happy to employ those skills in service of the artist's vision. And a note on that . . . when I hear about creative control struggles between artists and producers/mixers it baffles me. In my mind, the lead artist(s) holds the spiritual keys to the album inside them - there may be better or worse ways to execute ideas, but, ultimately, the final say is ALWAYS with the artist. It couldn't possibly be otherwise.

Lastly, I think there are some records that I can serve better than others. I'm drawn towards projects that have a spirit of adventurousness and openness (even if they are fully scored). Many of the records I've worked on feature classically-trained artists, but I'm extremely interested in continuing to work with artists from all practices including hip-hop, experimental pop, and various traditional musical practices from around the world.

Although I am co-founder/artistic director of New Amsterdam, I've produced albums for a number of different labels, including Merge, 37do3d/Secretly Canadian, and Nonesuch. Ultimately, part of my job is to figure out how to help projects reach the finish line and find the right home. I find the best way to start the whole process is with an open-ended conversation, so feel free to drop me a line if you'd like to talk...
